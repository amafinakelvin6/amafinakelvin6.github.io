The Return of a Civilization

Amafina Kelvin 2025 12 min read

Introduction

Historian Ada Palmer, through her historical scholarship, illustrates a crucial truth about technological progress: transformative systems do not suddenly appear in everyday life.[1] They diffuse gradually across generations, shaped by economics, culture, and material limitations. What later appears to be rapid change is often the visible outcome of long periods of experimentation and quiet refinement.

History reveals this pattern repeatedly, from the maturation of textile manufacturing during the Industrial Revolution to the slow transition from papyrus and parchment to wood-pulp paper. A technology may be technically possible long before it becomes economically viable, socially embedded, and infrastructurally dominant.

All industrial revolutions should therefore be understood not as isolated explosions of progress, but as continuing cycles of problem-solving. Palmer argues that industrialization is an ongoing process of inventing, reinventing, and re-reinventing production systems, with each phase accelerating the next.[1] Technological progress is cumulative and corrective: new tools emerge to resolve the inefficiencies of older systems while simultaneously creating new constraints that demand further innovation. Advancements in transportation, agriculture, manufacturing, and communication repeatedly reorganize society to overcome the limitations of earlier eras.

In her speculative series Terra Ignota, Palmer extends this pattern into imagined futures, portraying societies that continuously restructure themselves as new technologies reshape daily life.[2] Progress, she suggests, is often propelled by crisis. Innovations emerge not from comfort, but from the pressures created by economic strain, social disruption, and the unintended consequences of prior systems. Each technological layer becomes both a solution and the foundation for new complexity.

Crucially, Palmer challenges the popular "hockey-stick" narrative of human advancement — the belief that progress remained flat for centuries before suddenly accelerating in the modern era.[3] Instead, history is better understood as a curve already in motion, where each generation builds upon inherited knowledge, refining systems rather than replacing them outright. Industrial revolutions are therefore phases within a continuous problem-solving journey, not abrupt transformations detached from the past.

From this historical lens, innovation reveals itself not as sudden genius but as cumulative convergence. Breakthroughs emerge from countless failed experiments that progressively eliminate what does not work. Like phase transitions in chemistry, innovation has a threshold moment when enabling conditions align and transformation becomes unavoidable. I describe this as the innovation point: the stage in history when technical possibility, economic feasibility, and societal readiness intersect, making breakthrough inevitable rather than accidental. The innovation point is constant and recurring — a process that is not time-bound, but compounding.

This story is clearly observed in Africa's development cycle. What appears today as underdevelopment may be more accurately understood as another step in the evolution of our society — one shaped by interrupted cycles, misaligned external investment, and systems inherited from colonial priorities rather than continental needs. I find myself drawn to the idea that we may be approaching another innovation point. The conditions are assembling. What remains uncertain is whether the agents of transformation will be internal or external, and whether the philosophy guiding that transformation will serve African lives or external interests.

It is this perspective that shapes how I examine the infrastructure gaps confronting Africa today. Infrastructure failure is rarely sudden; it is the outcome of long cycles of technological diffusion, institutional delay, economic limitation, and fragmented coordination. Present deficiencies are therefore not isolated shortcomings, but symptoms of an unfinished developmental trajectory.

I do not write as a certified infrastructure specialist or policy scholar. I write as a citizen driven by urgency and responsibility — compelled by the desire to see African societies industrialize, prosper, and achieve structural independence. My perspective is shaped by a deep commitment to systems thinking, particularly the work of James Grier Miller, whose theory of living systems provides a framework for understanding how complex human environments function, fail, and adapt.[4] This intellectual foundation has led me into sustained inquiry: a search to understand the structural weaknesses limiting infrastructure development across the continent.

This inquiry revolves around several guiding questions:

  • What structural and historical forces created Africa's infrastructure gaps?
  • What phase of infrastructure evolution is now emerging on the continent?
  • Who are the true agents of systemic infrastructure transformation?
  • How prepared are African institutions and citizens to participate in this transformation?

These questions form the foundation of the mission at Langovest.[5] Our work is organized around investigating them with intellectual honesty, systemic analysis, and practical experimentation. We do not claim certainty. We operate with disciplined curiosity, aware that exploration often produces better questions before it produces definitive answers.

Human progress is iterative. Societies advance through cycles of trial and error, where each solution exposes new limitations. Every engineered system resolves existing constraints while simultaneously creating new dependencies and future vulnerabilities. Infrastructure development follows this same pattern: it is a continuous negotiation between present needs and emerging complexity.

For this reason, one task becomes unavoidable: before designing the future of African infrastructure, we must first understand its past.

Historical Overview of Africa's Infrastructure Development

As I try to examine the historical timeline of infrastructure development across Africa, I quickly realize how difficult it is to capture this story in a single section. The continent is vast, and each country carries its own history. Any serious attempt to understand infrastructure in Africa would require studying each nation independently. What I attempt here is more limited — a reflection on broad patterns that seem to appear across time.

Pre-Colonial Systems

When I look back into pre-colonial Africa, I am reminded that these were not modern nation states, but empires, kingdoms, and organized societies. As I examine this period, I find myself navigating between what is known and what is assumed. Historical data is not always complete due to the passage of time and the disruptions that followed later periods. But there is enough historical and archaeological evidence to suggest that these societies had organized systems and infrastructure, and in some cases remarkable levels of sophistication.

Examples such as the Benin Walls show evidence of large scale coordination and long term planning.[6] Sites such as Great Zimbabwe demonstrate advanced construction and settlement organization.[7] Cities like Timbuktu functioned as centers of trade, administration, and knowledge supported by regional trade routes.[8] Infrastructure existed, shaped by the needs of the societies that built it.

These systems were largely agrarian, inward facing, and designed around how people lived, how communities organized themselves, and how societies interacted with their environments rather than around industrial production or global trade integration.

Even the technologies of the time reflect this pattern. Ironworking, for example, developed gradually through experimentation across regions, improving tools for farming and settlement. These were not sudden breakthroughs but accumulated refinements built over generations, shaped by necessity, environment, and social organization.

Colonial Infrastructure Development

A clear shift began in the late nineteenth century, particularly following the Berlin Conference.[9] As European powers formalized colonial rule across African territories, infrastructure development accelerated under a different logic. Railways, ports, and major road networks began to appear at scale, though what stands out is the orientation of these systems — largely designed to move natural resources from inland production areas to coastal ports.

Examples such as the Uganda Railway and the Dakar-Niger Railway illustrate this outward logic.[10] Ports such as Apapa Port and Port of Mombasa became key gateways for export. These systems connected Africa to the global industrial economy, but not necessarily African societies to themselves, and as infrastructure expanded its purpose shifted — tied increasingly to extraction, trade, and external integration rather than internal development.[11]

Post-Colonial Transition and Early Independence

Following World War II, independence movements spread across the continent and newly formed African states inherited these infrastructure systems under conditions that made continuity difficult — much of what had been built was developed through foreign concession companies and administrative systems that were not deeply embedded within national institutions. After independence, many governments found themselves responsible for infrastructure they neither designed nor fully controlled.

In many cases, local technical capacity, institutional continuity, and financial systems necessary to maintain these assets were still developing. As a result, some infrastructure that could have formed the basis for continued economic development struggled to be sustained at the same level.[12]

At the same time, governments sought to industrialize rapidly. This led to ambitious national infrastructure projects intended to support industrial growth and economic independence. Examples include projects such as the Ajaokuta Steel Mill[11] and the Akosombo Dam,[12] which reflected a clear belief that large scale national infrastructure could accelerate development.

What stands out to me is not failure, but the difficulty of transition — from externally designed systems to internally managed ones, and from extraction focused networks to development oriented infrastructure.

Structural Adjustment and Development Partnerships

From around the 1980s onward, financial pressures led many African countries to work with institutions such as the International Monetary Fund and the Paris Club.[16] Infrastructure development during this period often took place within externally supported development frameworks, with investments expanding into social sectors such as healthcare, development programs, and institutional reforms.[17]

What becomes clearer in this phase is that infrastructure depends on governance capacity, institutional strength, and alignment with the realities of the societies it serves — and where these elements were misaligned, systems struggled regardless of funding or intent.

Continuity and Emerging Patterns

Across these phases, what I observe is less a story of isolated transformations and more a story of cycles shaped by changing priorities. Pre-colonial systems largely focused on internal realities. Colonial systems emphasized external connectivity. Post-independence focused on national industrial ambition. Later development cycles emphasized externally financed development frameworks. Each phase responded to the pressures of its time, and in doing so, shaped the conditions that made the next phase necessary — a pattern that continues into the present.

The State of Africa's Infrastructure Right Now

Looking at the more recent decades, I see what feels like another infrastructure cycle forming — one shaped not only by national governments or traditional development partners, but by renewed global interest in African infrastructure. Initiatives such as China's Belt and Road Initiative,[18] the European Union's Global Gateway,[19] and American-led efforts such as the Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment[20] all point toward a new phase of engagement focused on transport, digital systems, energy transition, and climate infrastructure.

When I examine these developments side by side, I cannot help but notice a familiar structural pattern. Much of the large-scale infrastructure emphasis still appears oriented toward trade facilitation, logistics corridors, ports, and connectivity that supports Africa's integration into global markets.[21] The intentions, language, financing models, and geopolitical context today may all differ from earlier periods, but at a structural level the orientation sometimes feels recognisable.[22]

Some observers have even described this moment as a form of a "third scramble for Africa"[23] — not in the territorial sense of the past, but in the competition for access to strategic resources that now power the global digital and green economy. This phrase is used here as an analytical observation — a pattern that seems worth examining rather than a political characterisation.

As global demand grows for lithium, cobalt, rare earth minerals, and other materials essential to batteries, artificial intelligence hardware, and clean energy systems, infrastructure connected to extraction, logistics, and export corridors continues to attract major capital, and this makes sense from a global economic perspective. But when I step back and look at the internal realities of many African cities, I also notice something else existing at the same time — in some cases, major investments in ports, transport corridors, or export infrastructure exist alongside unreliable electricity supply, limited access to clean water, strained healthcare systems, or rapidly expanding informal settlements.[24]

This is an observation of two realities existing side by side: infrastructure that connects Africa outward continues to expand, while infrastructure that supports everyday life internally often develops more slowly. Yet Africa's own context is shifting rapidly — societies are becoming increasingly urban, cities are expanding at historic rates, a young population is generating new economic pressures and opportunities, the diaspora is becoming more economically and intellectually engaged, and digital connectivity is reshaping how Africans learn, trade, collaborate, and build businesses.

These changes suggest that the next infrastructure questions may not only be about how Africa connects to the world, but also about how infrastructure supports how Africans live within their own cities and communities.

What Does It Mean to Build Infrastructure for Africans?

As I reflect on the longer arc of development across the last two centuries, one question keeps returning to me: what does it mean to build infrastructure for Africans? The question is whether infrastructure can be designed around the daily rhythms of African life — not only around the movement of goods, the mechanics of production, or the logic of outward connectivity.

When I think about everyday living, I think about reliable access to clean water. Stable electricity for homes and small businesses. Functional healthcare systems. Educational institutions that truly serve students. Digital connectivity that allows participation in a modern economy. Mobility systems that make daily movement predictable and safe.

I do not claim to have a prescription or a magic solution. These reflections come only from observation and from an attempt to understand how infrastructure systems evolve over time. And as I think about this more carefully, I also realize something important: what I am describing may not be entirely new.

If we look back into pre-colonial African societies, we see infrastructure largely shaped around the needs of the societies that built it. Systems were designed around agriculture, settlement patterns, trade within regions, and the practical needs of daily life — infrastructure organised primarily around inward realities rather than external ones.

What changed over time was not simply the existence of infrastructure, but its orientation. Colonial development emphasized extraction and external integration. Post-independence development emphasized industrial scale and national ambition. Later development cycles emphasized externally financed development programs.

The argument is for balance — that industrial infrastructure and global connectivity remain essential, but equal emphasis now needs to be placed on the systems that support internal realities. In that sense, what I find myself describing may simply be another turn in the infrastructure development cycle — one where the focus begins to move again toward human environments and daily living, but this time with access to technologies that earlier societies did not have.

Today we have decentralized energy systems, modular construction approaches, digital coordination platforms, mobile infrastructure models, and new financing approaches that allow infrastructure to be delivered faster and closer to where people actually live. The real question is whether we now have the tools to do what societies have always tried to do — build infrastructure around how people actually live — but this time with far greater capability.

Two Infrastructure Models

I find it useful to organize these observations into two broad models — not as rigid categories, but as patterns that describe where most infrastructure attention has historically been directed, and where I believe additional focus is necessary. For simplicity, I describe these as the Industrial Infrastructure Model and the Livelihood Infrastructure Model. This distinction exists purely to organize how infrastructure can be understood based on the function it serves and where its primary impact is felt, with the two models serving as complementary lenses, each illuminating a different dimension of the same development challenge.

The Industrial Infrastructure Model

The first model is what most infrastructure development across Africa has historically followed. This model focuses on infrastructure built to enable industrialization, economic coordination, and national scale production, prioritizing systems that allow goods, energy, and resources to move efficiently across regions and into global markets.

Typical examples include highways and transport corridors, rail networks, seaports and logistics hubs, national power generation and transmission systems, refineries and processing plants, large dams and energy projects, and industrial zones and manufacturing clusters.

The primary function of this model is economic enablement. It allows production to scale, supports trade, enables industrial activity, and integrates economies into regional and global systems. This model has played an important role in Africa's development — connecting regions, enabling commerce, and supporting national economic ambitions — with its primary orientation around how economies function.

The Livelihood Infrastructure Model

The second model is what I describe as the Livelihood Infrastructure Model. This model operates as a complementary layer to the industrial model, focused on a fundamentally different question: how people live. Where the industrial model organizes around economic function, the livelihood model organizes around human environments — the environments where people spend most of their time and where daily life unfolds continuously.

These environments include residential communities, hospitals, university campuses, markets, transport terminals, and dense urban neighborhoods.

The primary function of this model is to support the conditions that make daily living possible. This includes infrastructure that improves access to clean water, food systems, healthcare access, education support environments, sanitation, local mobility, and digital connectivity. This model tends to favor infrastructure that is closer to where people live, faster to deploy, modular or mobile where possible, and practical in improving daily living conditions. If the industrial model enables economic growth, the livelihood model focuses on enabling improvements in standard of living — together describing two different but necessary dimensions of infrastructure development: one focused on production and coordination, the other focused on living conditions and human environments.

Conclusion: The Rebalancing Act

If I return to where I began — with the idea that progress is cumulative rather than sudden — then Africa's infrastructure story begins to look less like failure and more like a sequence of responses, each phase attempting to solve the most visible limitation of its time while introducing new constraints that required further adaptation. Colonial infrastructure addressed the limitations of global trade integration. Post-independence infrastructure attempted to address national coordination and industrial ambition. Later development efforts responded to basic human survival challenges such as health and institutional capacity. Each phase solved one limitation while revealing the next.

Seen this way, what we are witnessing today may not be a break from the past, but another transition point. The distinction is one of intention — livelihood infrastructure already exists across our cities and communities in the form of water systems, markets, hospitals, educational institutions, and connectivity, but what appears to be emerging is the opportunity to treat these systems with the same planning rigour, investment logic, and long-term thinking historically applied to ports, corridors, and power systems — as part of how development itself is structured.

Because when viewed through the lens of productivity, livelihood infrastructure reveals another role. Infrastructure that improves daily living conditions also improves economic participation. Healthier populations work more consistently. Stable food systems reduce vulnerability. Reliable education systems strengthen human capital. Connectivity expands opportunity. In this sense, livelihood infrastructure does not compete with industrial development — it strengthens the human foundation that industrial development depends on.

Which is why this moment feels less like a proposal for change and more like what I earlier described as an innovation point — a moment where technical possibility, social need, and developmental readiness begin to align, not because something entirely new has appeared, but because conditions now allow us to think differently about what already exists.

The next phase of Africa's infrastructure development is about balance — industrial infrastructure builds economic capacity, livelihood infrastructure strengthens societal capacity, and long-term development depends on advancing both with equal intentionality. The opportunity is to direct as much thought, coordination, and seriousness toward the infrastructure that improves daily living as has historically been directed toward infrastructure that enables industrial-scale growth — building forward from what exists, rather than around it.

Because if infrastructure ultimately shapes how societies function, then the next phase in Africa's infrastructure revolution will not be defined by what we build, but by what we finally decide to balance.
